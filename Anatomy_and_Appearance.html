Anatomy & Appearance

Nicknamed the Pygmy Gourami due to its unusually small size for being a gourami, this tiny fish grows to a maximum length of around 1.6 inches (about 4 cm) and shares a similar body composition to that of a female or juvenile Betta (Betta splendens). However, despite that nickname, this gourami is more widely known as the Sparkling Gourami due to the uniquely iridescent colors found across its body, fins, and eyes when viewed under sufficient lighting.

This diminutive fish features a rather muted light brown body with patterns of reddish-brown and iridescent light blue, with that iridescent light blue not only being present on its body but also on its fins and eyes. Depending on the lighting, the patterns on this fish may shimmer in hues of blue, green, and red as it swims.

Like other members of the gourami family, the Sparkling Gourami has a labyrinth organ, a lung-like organ which enables the fish to breathe in gulps of oxygen from above the water's surface. This trait has been essential for this gourami's survival since they originate from slower moving rivers and ponds that contain minimal amounts of dissolved oxygen. Thanks to its labyrinth organ, this fish also has the ability to create bubble nests for spawning and raising their young.

Aside from having a unique iridescent coloration and labyrinth organ, these fish also possess a unique form of communication. These rather small fish have the ability to make an audible croaking noise through use of specialized adaptations of their pectoral fins. Though not extremely loud, the noise is audible enough that one can hear it if they happen to be in the same room. These noises are produced by both sexes during mating and through the establishment of dominance hierarchies among males.
